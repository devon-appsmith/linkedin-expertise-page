There's a specific irony in spending years writing about AI for companies building AI before you actually start using it for anything serious. I did this. My workflow was: open a doc, type words, drink coffee. ChatGPT launched, I played with it, wrote some content about it, and kept doing what I'd been doing. The shift came through accumulation — useful for a quick gut-check, then a user interview, then one afternoon running market analysis and reverse-engineering a competitor's social strategy from screenshots. Work that would've eaten a full day.

I have limited brain power. By 3 pm, I'm basically a jellyfish. 🪼 But AI lets me squeeze a little more juice out of those drooling afternoons.

What I've figured out — mostly the hard way — is that the tool is only half of it. Knowing what to ask, and giving enough context that the answer is actually useful, is a learned skill. It's not mystical "prompt engineering." It's briefing a colleague properly. My clearest window into how these models actually work came from building a trivia game — I needed questions where every answer contained a type of cheese. Any person gets the pattern instantly. The LLM melted down.

My guess is that the prompt triggers a breakdown in the LLM's tokenization/semantic search process, where it starts scrambling to find similarities to the target at the level of a token, not the word or concept itself. At least that explains why it thinks "Confederate" has "feta" in it.

Claude Code specifically changed what I'm able to ship. I've used it to build the mechanics of a side project, fix bugs same-day, prototype growth ideas I'd otherwise have needed to spec out for a developer — or just not do. The skill isn't coding. It's knowing what you want, and recognizing when the output's gone sideways. If you've been doing knowledge work for any length of time, you already have both of those things.

Fortunately, I had my buddy Claude Code on call to push a fix by the afternoon. My friends called me out for my goof. Your customers are just going to look somewhere else.
