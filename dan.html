📈 Demand Generation · 42 posts · 2017–2025

Stop generating demand.
Start capturing intent.

Written from real posts, not from scratch. This narrative was synthesized from Dan's LinkedIn writing across five companies and eight years. Original posts are embedded throughout — you'll recognize them.

I've run demand generation at five startups, and the lesson that took me longest to accept is this: you can't create demand that doesn't already exist. You can educate. You can build category awareness. You can grow a market slowly over years with the right content and community play. But when you have an 18-month runway and a VP of Sales asking why the pipeline is thin, "creating demand" is a luxury you don't have. What you actually need is to find the people who already have the problem you solve and get in front of them before your competitors do.

March 4, 2018 · 🌐
Unpopular opinion: most "demand gen" programs at early-stage startups are actually just brand programs with a pipeline number attached to them. Real demand gen is ruthless about intent. It says: who is in-market RIGHT NOW, what are they searching, what events do they attend, what communities are they in — and how do we get there first? At Stackform we stopped running broad awareness plays in month 4 and went full intent-based. G2 reviews, specific job titles + keywords on LinkedIn, retargeting anyone who visited competitor sites. Pipeline went up 3x in a quarter. Spending went down 20%. If your demand gen program can't tell me who it's targeting this week, it's not demand gen. It's hope. #demandgen #b2bmarketing #startupmarketing

At Routebase we were burning $80K a month on top-of-funnel content and seeing maybe 12% of it touch any deal that closed. The content was good — genuinely good. But it was attracting the wrong people, or attracting the right people too early, before they had budget authority or an active project. The fix wasn't to make better content. The fix was to understand where in the buying journey each piece of content was useful, and stop expecting early-stage educational content to drive pipeline directly. Once we stopped measuring the wrong thing, we could actually invest in the right mix.

The MQL debate consumed a truly embarrassing number of hours of my career. At three different companies I was handed an MQL definition written by someone who'd never sat in a sales call — usually a point threshold on form fills and page views that was optimized to make marketing look good, not to generate revenue. The MQLs flowed. The sales team ignored them. We'd argue about lead quality. Nothing changed. What finally worked was abandoning MQLs in favor of a buying committee model: who in the target account was engaged, at what depth, and did the company profile match? Messier to build. Much harder to game.

I abolished MQLs at Routebase six months ago. Here's what happened. Sales stopped ignoring marketing leads. Not because the leads got better overnight — but because we changed what we were handing them. Instead of "this person downloaded an ebook and visited pricing," we gave them: account-level engagement score, job title of most engaged contact, specific pages viewed, time since first touch. Same data. Different framing. The rep could actually do something with it. MQLs were a reporting construct that made marketers feel good and gave sales reps nothing to work with. Abolish the MQL. Build a signal. #revops #demandgen #b2bsales

By the time I got to Veloxa, I'd started thinking about demand generation as a flywheel rather than a funnel. Funnels imply a one-time journey. Flywheels imply that momentum compounds. The best demand gen programs I've run have a content engine that builds SEO authority over 12–18 months, a community layer that generates organic advocacy, and a paid layer that accelerates in-market accounts while the organic motion scales. Each layer feeds the others. Cut one and the others slow down. The mistake most teams make is treating paid as the whole program — it's the accelerant, not the fuel.

We 3x'd qualified pipeline at Veloxa in Q2 without increasing paid spend. Thread on how 👇 1/ We stopped trying to generate demand and started capturing it. Obvious in theory, hard in practice. 2/ Built a 60-topic SEO cluster around our category's actual buying questions. Not vanity terms. The stuff prospects search at 11pm when their current tool is failing them. 3/ Added a community channel (Slack) for practitioners in our space. Not to sell — to be useful. 400 members in 90 days. 22% became pipeline within 6 months. 4/ Used paid ONLY to retarget engaged accounts (visited 3+ pages or was in community). CPL went up 40%. Pipeline quality went up 3x. Worth it. 5/ Told sales reps to stop cold outbounding accounts that haven't shown intent. Hard conversation. Revenue up 28% in the next quarter. The flywheel is slow to start. But once it's spinning, you can't stop it. What's working for your team right now? #demandgen #contentmarketing #b2bmarketing

Now at Meridian AI, the environment is different again — AI-native tools, a market that's simultaneously crowded and confused, buyers who've been burned by hype cycles and are more skeptical than ever. The demand gen playbook I'd built over the previous four companies needed recalibration. What I'm learning: in emerging categories, you really do have to invest in education before you can capture intent, because the intent signals don't exist yet. The buyers don't know what to search for. The community hasn't formed. The category terms aren't established. You're back to the top of the funnel — but this time you know exactly how long the bottom takes to fill, and you can plan for it instead of panicking.
